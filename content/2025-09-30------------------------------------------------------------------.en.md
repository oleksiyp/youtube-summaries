### The Making of a Financial Titan: Larry Fink's Rise and Fall in the 1980s

- **1980s New York** represented peak American capitalism under Ronald Reagan's economic policies
- Reagan's "Reaganomics" included three key elements: massive tax cuts for the wealthy, deregulation of banks (dismantling Glass-Steagall protections), and aggressive inflation fighting through Fed Chairman Paul Volcker
- Volcker raised interest rates to 20% to kill inflation, causing severe recession but ultimately succeeding
- The falling interest rate environment created a "giant escalator" for bond traders—buying bonds at high rates and watching them appreciate as rates fell
- **Larry Fink emerged as a "quant"**—a new breed of mathematical trader using computers and formulas rather than gut instinct
- Fink pioneered mortgage-backed securities, buying thousands of home loans from banks and repackaging them for investors
- At age 31, Fink became the youngest managing director in his company's history
- In early 1986, Fink's mathematical model predicted falling interest rates, so he bet everything—but **rates went up instead, losing $100 million**
- This catastrophic failure transformed Fink from "genius" to "toxic manager" and became his defining trauma

### Building Aladdin: From Failure to Revolutionary Risk Management

- After his 1986 disaster, Fink fundamentally changed his philosophy from "how much can we earn?" to **"how much can we lose?"**
- Fink assembled a core team: Robert Kapito (operations), Susan Wagner (mathematical modeling), Ben Golub (programming), and Ralph Schlosstein (connections)
- Schlosstein connected Fink with **Stephen Schwarzman of Blackstone**, who agreed to let Fink's team operate as a division
- The team built **Aladdin** (Asset, Liability, Debt and Derivative Investment Network)—a revolutionary risk analysis system
- Unlike previous models that analyzed past data, Aladdin could **model all possible future scenarios**: oil price shocks, military coups, pandemics, bank failures
- Aladdin became Fink's "sacred grail"—a system for total risk control
- The breakthrough came in 1994 when **General Electric** hired them to analyze a $7 billion portfolio of complex securities no one else could evaluate
- Aladdin successfully dissected the portfolio, leading GE to transfer asset management to Fink's team
- By the mid-1990s, the division managed nearly $24 billion and generated tens of millions in profit
